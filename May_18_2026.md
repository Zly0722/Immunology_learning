# Antigen Presentation Pathways

## 1. Core idea

T cells do not recognize free or soluble antigens directly.

Instead, T cell receptors (TCRs) recognize short antigenic peptides only when those peptides are displayed on the cell surface by Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) molecules.

In humans, MHC molecules are called Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) molecules. Therefore, in human immunology, the terms MHC and HLA are often used interchangeably.

---

## 2. B cells vs T cells

| Feature | B cells | T cells |
|---|---|---|
| Antigen recognition | Can recognize free antigens directly | Recognize only MHC-peptide complexes |
| Receptor | B cell receptor / membrane-bound immunoglobulin | T cell receptor |
| Antigen form | Native protein, polysaccharide, toxin, viral surface antigen, etc. | Processed peptide bound to MHC |
| Main role | Detect extracellular antigens directly | Inspect what is being displayed on cell surfaces |

---

## 3. What does “T cells look into other cells” mean?

T cells cannot literally see inside another cell.

Instead, intracellular proteins are degraded into short peptides. These peptides are loaded onto MHC molecules and transported to the cell surface.

By scanning MHC-peptide complexes, T cells can indirectly monitor what is happening inside a cell.

This is especially important for detecting:

- virus-infected cells
- tumor cells
- abnormal intracellular protein expression
- foreign transplanted tissue

---

## 4. What is MHC?

MHC stands for Major Histocompatibility Complex.

Its main function is to display antigenic peptides on the cell surface for inspection by T cells.

MHC molecules are highly polymorphic, meaning that different individuals carry different allelic versions of MHC genes. These different MHC molecules bind different sets of peptides.

This diversity is useful for population-level defense against pathogens, but it also creates problems in transplantation because another person’s MHC molecules may be recognized as foreign.

---

## 5. T cell receptor structure

The T cell receptor is usually composed of two transmembrane polypeptide chains:

- TCR α chain
- TCR β chain

These chains belong to the immunoglobulin superfamily.

The variable regions of the TCR α and β chains form the antigen-binding site.

TCR diversity is generated by gene segment rearrangement during T cell development in the thymus. This process is called V(D)J recombination.

---

## 6. TCR and CD3 complex

The TCR α and β chains mainly recognize the MHC-peptide complex.

However, they cannot efficiently transmit intracellular signals by themselves.

Therefore, the TCR associates with the CD3 complex, which includes:

- CD3 γ
- CD3 δ
- CD3 ε
- CD3 ζ

The CD3 complex converts antigen recognition into intracellular signaling and initiates T cell activation.

---

## 7. MHC class I vs MHC class II

| Feature | MHC class I | MHC class II |
|---|---|---|
| Main expression site | Almost all nucleated cells | Professional antigen-presenting cells |
| Main cell types | Most nucleated cells | Dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells |
| Antigen source | Endogenous antigen | Exogenous antigen |
| Antigen origin | Proteins synthesized inside the cell | Proteins taken up from outside the cell |
| Recognized by | CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells | CD4⁺ helper T cells |
| Main immune function | Kill infected or abnormal cells | Coordinate and help immune responses |

---

## 8. MHC class I pathway

MHC class I molecules mainly present endogenous antigens.

Endogenous antigens come from proteins synthesized inside the cell.

Examples include:

- viral proteins made inside infected cells
- abnormal proteins produced by tumor cells
- normal self proteins produced inside healthy cells

These proteins are degraded into short peptides. The peptides are loaded onto MHC class I molecules and displayed on the cell surface.

CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells recognize MHC I-peptide complexes.

If the displayed peptide indicates infection or abnormality, CD8⁺ T cells can kill the presenting cell.
