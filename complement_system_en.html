§ 1 – Core Concept

What Is the Complement System?

The complement system is one of the oldest and most important parts of the immune system. It is not made of cells. Instead, it consists of more than 30 different proteins that float through body fluids, silently waiting.

When these proteins detect signs of danger — microbial surfaces, antibody signals, or spontaneous activation — they switch on. What follows is a rapid, self-amplifying protein cascade that can mark invaders, call for help, and sometimes destroy them outright.

"A group of immune proteins that mark, damage, recruit against, and directly kill invaders."

It belongs to the innate immune system — the body's fast, general-purpose defense that acts before any specific antibody or T cell is ready.

⚡
Lightning Fast

🔁
Self-Amplifying
One signal triggers thousands of protein activations through cascades
🤝

🎯

Targets foreign surfaces while body cells carry protective shields
§ 4 – Activation Mechanism

Silent by Design — Explosive When Triggered

Complement proteins are everywhere in body fluids, yet most are completely inert until activated. They work through conformational changes — shape shifts that unlock new binding capabilities. This "off by default" design is a safety feature: an always-on complement would attack the body's own tissues.

🔒 Inactive complement protein
Floating harmlessly in body fluid
⚡ Activation signal received
Microbial surface / antibody / spontaneous
🔄 Protein changes shape
New binding sites exposed
✅ Active — can bind · recruit · attack · amplify
⚠️
Complement proteins must be kept dormant because they are inherently dangerous. Without proper control, they would attack healthy tissue — a phenomenon seen in several autoimmune diseases.
§ 5 – Key Mechanism

The Cascade: One Signal → Thousands

The defining feature of the complement system is the cascade. One activated protein activates several others, which activate even more. A tiny spark becomes a firestorm in seconds.

🌊
This makes the complement system fast and explosive. Once it latches onto a foreign surface, it rapidly multiplies and coats the invader — all before a single immune cell arrives.
§ 6 – 9 · Central Protein

C3: The Linchpin of the Entire System

Among all complement proteins, C3 is the most central. When activated, it cleaves into two fragments that perform completely different — yet equally vital — roles.

FragmentMain RoleHow It Behaves
C3a Alarm signal — attracts and activates immune cells Diffuses away from the infection site like a chemical distress flare; functions similarly to cytokines
C3b Opsonin — sticks to microbes and marks them for destruction Binds tightly to the bacterial surface, changes shape, recruits more complement proteins, and seeds an amplification platform
🔑
C3 is the pivot of the entire system. It connects alarm signaling, microbial tagging, cascade amplification, and the assembly of the killing weapon — all from one cleavage event.
§ 8 – Opsonization

Opsonization: Making Bacteria Irresistible to Eat

Bacteria often have surfaces that actively repel immune cells or are simply too slippery to grab. Opsonization solves this problem.

When C3b and other complement proteins coat a bacterium, they act like molecular handles. Macrophages and neutrophils carry complement receptors (CR1, CR3) that grip these handles, pulling the bacterium in for destruction.

🍽️
The word opsonize comes from the Greek opsonein — "to prepare food." Complement literally seasons bacteria to make them more appetizing to phagocytes.
§ 7 – Amplification

The C3b Amplification Loop

Once C3b attaches to a bacterial surface, it changes shape and begins recruiting more complement proteins. Together they form a C3 convertase — a molecular machine that produces even more C3b. This creates a runaway positive-feedback loop.

⏱️
Within seconds, thousands of C3b molecules can coat a single bacterium — transforming it from a stealthy invader into an unmistakable target.
§ 10 – Terminal Weapon

Membrane Attack Complex (MAC)

The most dramatic complement weapon is the Membrane Attack Complex — a ring-shaped protein assembly that literally punches a hole through a bacterial membrane. The hole cannot be repaired, and the bacterium loses osmotic control and dies.

MAC is most effective against Gram-negative bacteria, whose thin peptidoglycan walls leave the outer membrane exposed to complement attack.

🔴
The pore formed by MAC cannot be self-repaired. Fluid rushes in, ions leak out, and the bacterium either bursts (lysis) or loses the ability to maintain its internal environment — either way, it dies.
§ 12 – Activation Route

The Alternative Pathway

Of the three complement activation routes, the alternative pathway is uniquely powerful: it requires no antibodies and can ignite spontaneously on any microbial surface it touches. It is the backbone of early innate defense.

C3 undergoes low-level spontaneous hydrolysis — always ticking over
C3b is released and randomly contacts a nearby surface
On self cells: regulatory proteins destroy C3b immediately → no reaction
On microbes: C3b survives and binds tightly → pathway ignites
Factor B binds C3b → Factor D cleaves Factor B → C3 convertase forms
C3 convertase cleaves more C3 → amplification loop begins
💥 Bacterium coated in C3b → opsonized + MAC assembly triggered
🔑
The key insight: the alternative pathway distinguishes self from non-self not by recognition, but by regulation. Microbes lack the regulatory proteins that protect host cells — so complement attacks them and spares us.
§ 13 – Cooperation

Complement Doesn't Fight Alone

Complement is one layer in a multi-wave defense. It synergizes with every other component of innate immunity — and amplifies signals that eventually recruit adaptive immunity as well.

§ 14 – Regulation

Powerful and Dangerous: The Need for Control

Complement proteins can activate spontaneously. Without robust inhibition, they would attack healthy tissues — a problem seen in diseases like paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria and atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome. The immune system must walk a razor's edge.

✅ Must Activate Fast Enough

Even a small wound can let bacteria replicate faster than a slow immune response can control. Speed is survival — delays of minutes can mean the difference between containment and sepsis.

⚠️ Must Stay Controlled Enough

Runaway complement activity attacks host tissues. Regulators like Factor H, Factor I, CD55 (DAF), and CD59 constantly dismantle complement that lands on self cells.

Regulatory ProteinWhat It DoesWhere Active
Factor HBinds C3b on host cells; accelerates degradationFluid phase + host cell surfaces
Factor ICleaves and inactivates C3b (with cofactors)Requires Factor H or MCP
CD55 / DAFAccelerates decay of C3 convertase on self cellsHost cell membranes only
CD59Blocks MAC assembly on self cell membranesHost cell membranes only
⚖️
Pathogens that survive complement attacks have evolved countermeasures — they steal host regulatory proteins, secrete inhibitors, or coat themselves in materials that mimic self-cell surfaces.
§ 11 – Limitations

Complement vs. Viruses: A Harder Battle

Complement excels against bacteria because their large surfaces are easily coated. Viruses present a harder problem for three key reasons:

🔬
Extremely Small
Tiny surface area means complement proteins rarely make random contact before the virus enters a cell
🕵️
Hide Inside Cells
Viruses replicate intracellularly — safe from extracellular complement in body fluids
🏃
Move Quickly
Cell-to-cell spread bypasses extracellular complement entirely
🎭
Steal Host Proteins
Some viruses bud with host CD55/CD59 — essentially wearing our own complement shields
🧬
Fighting viruses requires a different toolkit: interferons, NK cells, cytotoxic T lymphocytes, and neutralizing antibodies — all of which take days to fully deploy. Complement can help intercept free virions, but it is not the primary antiviral defense.
§ 16 — Big Picture Summary

The Complete Picture

The complement system is an ancient, protein-based defense network. Its central logic is elegantly simple:

🚨 Danger detected — microbial surface exposed
⚡ C3 cleaves → C3a (alarm) + C3b (tag) released
📣 C3a alerts immune cells — recruitment begins
🏷️ C3b coats bacterium → amplification loop ignites
🍽️ Opsonization — phagocytes engulf the tagged bacterium
💥 MAC assembles → membrane pierced → bacterium lysed
✅ Infection cleared — homeostasis restored
Key Vocabulary

Essential Terms at a Glance

Complement system
Group of 30+ immune proteins that help detect, mark, recruit against, and destroy invaders
Innate immunity
Fast, non-specific defense present from birth — acts before adaptive immunity
C3
The central complement protein; cleaves into C3a and C3b upon activation
C3a
Alarm fragment of C3; acts as an anaphylatoxin that attracts and activates immune cells
C3b
Opsonin fragment of C3; binds covalently to microbial surfaces and seeds amplification
Cascade
Chain reaction where each activated protein activates many others — exponential amplification
Opsonization
Coating an invader with proteins (C3b, antibodies) that phagocyte receptors can grab
Phagocyte
Immune cell that engulfs and digests pathogens; includes macrophages and neutrophils
Macrophage
Long-lived innate sentinel that detects, engulfs, and signals danger via cytokines
Neutrophil
Short-lived, rapidly recruited killer cell — aggressive but can damage surrounding tissue
Cytokine
Signaling protein released by immune cells to coordinate the response across tissues
Inflammation
Local emergency response — vasodilation + increased permeability floods tissue with immune players
MAC
Membrane Attack Complex — ring-shaped complement weapon that punches lethal pores in membranes
C3 convertase
Enzyme complex that cleaves C3 in bulk — the engine of the amplification loop
Alternative pathway
Antibody-independent complement route that ignites on any unprotected foreign surface
Anaphylatoxin
Complement fragments (C3a, C5a) that trigger mast cell degranulation and immune cell recruitment
